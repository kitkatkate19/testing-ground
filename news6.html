Data Science in the Classroom:
Why Olympiad-Style Competition Works

Arjun Sharma
Data Science Programme Adviser, KBI HK
21 February 2026
6 min read

In October 2023, twenty-four teams gathered at CUHK Business School for the first IDSOL HK qualifying round. The challenge: analyse an unseen dataset — Hong Kong MTR ridership patterns — produce a meaningful insight, and present their findings live within a four-hour window. No prior knowledge of the dataset. No textbook to guide them. Just the data, their tools, and four hours.

The standard of work that emerged was, frankly, surprising — even to the judges. Teams that had never worked with transport data before produced regression models, anomaly detection analyses, and clustering outputs that would have been creditable in a graduate research context. One team's analysis of post-pandemic ridership recovery across different line types was subsequently cited by a judge in a conference paper.

That outcome did not happen by accident. It happened because the olympiad format does something that classroom instruction, however excellent, cannot easily replicate: it creates the conditions under which students discover what they are actually capable of.

"The unseen dataset format is deliberately uncomfortable. That discomfort is pedagogically intentional — it is the closest we can get to replicating real professional data science conditions in a student competition context."

— Arjun Sharma, Data Science Programme Adviser, KBI HK

The Limitations of Coursework-Based Assessment

Before examining why olympiad-style competition works, it is worth being precise about what conventional data science education does well — and where its limitations lie.

University data science courses, at their best, do an excellent job of introducing students to the conceptual foundations: statistical inference, machine learning paradigms, model evaluation, visualisation principles. A well-designed undergraduate data science programme will produce graduates who understand the theory and can apply standard techniques to well-structured problems.

The limitations emerge when students face real-world conditions: messy data, ambiguous problem definitions, time constraints, and the absence of a marking rubric that tells them what "correct" looks like. These are the conditions under which data science is actually practised — and they are almost entirely absent from standard coursework assessment.

Coursework assignments typically provide a dataset, a set of questions, and a rubric. The student's task is to produce the expected output. This is a useful exercise in applying known techniques. But it does not develop the judgment to know which technique is appropriate, the creativity to find a non-obvious angle, or the resilience to handle an approach that fails partway through and needs to be abandoned. Those skills develop under pressure, with real stakes and genuine uncertainty.

What the Olympiad Format Adds

IDSOL HK's qualifying format is designed around five principles that distinguish it from conventional assessment:

  • Unseen data. Teams receive the dataset at the start of the competition window. There is no opportunity to prepare domain-specific knowledge in advance. This forces teams to rely on transferable analytical skills rather than topic-specific preparation.
  • Open-ended problem framing. Rather than being given specific questions to answer, teams are asked to produce "meaningful, defensible insights" from the data. What counts as meaningful is a judgment call — exactly the kind of judgment professional data scientists make every day.
  • Time-bounded delivery. The four-hour window is not arbitrary. It mirrors the kind of time pressure found in hackathons, client presentations, and real-world analytical briefs. Learning to scope a problem appropriately within a time constraint is a skill that takes practice.
  • Live presentation with expert questioning. Teams present their findings to a panel of judges who ask follow-up questions. This tests understanding at a depth that written submissions alone cannot reach — you cannot cite a formula you don't understand when a judge asks you to explain why you chose it.
  • Peer calibration. Competing alongside other high-ability teams provides a calibration point that classroom settings rarely offer. Seeing what peers at your level are capable of is a powerful motivator — and occasionally a humbling corrective.

The Learning That Happens Before the Competition

It would be a mistake to focus exclusively on the competition day itself. The most significant learning in IDSOL HK — as with any olympiad — happens in the preparation period. Teams preparing for IDSOL HK 2026 will, over the preceding months, work through more datasets, more analytical frameworks, and more presentation rehearsals than they would encounter in a typical academic semester. They will do this voluntarily, motivated by the prospect of a meaningful performance context.

This is one of the most underappreciated aspects of competition-based learning: the competition is the visible output, but the preparation is where most of the learning happens. And unlike coursework, where the learning stops at the deadline, competition preparation has no natural endpoint — teams continue refining their approach as long as they have time and motivation.

Why This Matters for Hong Kong Specifically

Hong Kong's university system produces strong technical graduates. HKU, CUHK, HKUST, and PolyU all run competitive data science and statistics programmes. The issue is not technical competence — it is the gap between technical competence and applied judgment.

Employers in Hong Kong's financial services, logistics, and healthcare sectors increasingly report that graduate hires arrive with strong theoretical foundations but limited experience of working with ambiguous, real-world problems under time pressure. This is not a criticism of the universities — it reflects a genuine structural gap between what academic assessment optimises for and what professional practice requires.

Olympiad-style competition does not replace university education. But it fills a specific gap that university education is not designed to address: the gap between knowing how to apply a technique and knowing when to apply it, why it is the right choice, and how to communicate the results to someone who wasn't in the room when you ran the model.

"Every team that competes in IDSOL HK walks away having done something they didn't think they could do. That experience of discovering your own capability is not something you can teach in a lecture."

— Arjun Sharma, KBI HK Data Science Adviser

Getting Involved in IDSOL HK 2026

The IDSOL track is open to all ETO HK 2026 participants. Teams of two to five can compete in either the Schoolers Stream (secondary school students) or the Students Stream (tertiary). Mixed-school and mixed-institution teams are welcome and encouraged — some of the strongest analytical teams we have seen have brought together students with different domain knowledge (statistics, computer science, domain expertise) who would not have met through their institutions alone.

If you are an educator reading this: the best thing you can do for your students is encourage them to register. The preparation process alone — working through datasets, building presentations, rehearsing their reasoning under pressure — will develop skills that their coursework is not currently developing. The competition day is a bonus.
