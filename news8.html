Nine Years of KBI Hong Kong:
What We Have Learned About Serious Tech Education

Dr. Michelle Lam
Chapter Director, KBI Hong Kong
5 December 2025
9 min read

In December 2016, a small group of Hong Kong students gathered in a seminar room at CUHK to discuss whether the city needed its own chapter of the Königsberger Bridges Institute. The conversation lasted three hours. By the end of it, we had agreed that it did — and that we were the ones who were going to build it.

Nine years later, I am writing this as KBI Hong Kong prepares for its most ambitious programme cycle yet: three competition tracks, two participation streams, an expanded community programme, a restructured committee, and our first dedicated sponsorship strategy. The organisation looks almost nothing like that 2016 seminar room conversation. And yet the core of what we were trying to do then is exactly what we are still trying to do now.

This article is a reflection on what nine years of building a serious technology education organisation in Hong Kong has taught us — what worked, what did not, and what we would do differently if we were starting again.

"The students who take KBI HK seriously are not the ones who want an activity to put on their university application. They are the ones who want to understand something real. Those are the students a serious organisation is built for."

— Dr. Michelle Lam, Chapter Director, KBI Hong Kong

Where We Started

The early years of KBI HK were characterised by scarcity and enthusiasm in roughly equal measure. We had no budget, no venue, no track record, and no certainty that Hong Kong students would engage with olympiad-style technology competition at all. What we had was a small committee of people who believed deeply that Hong Kong's technology education ecosystem had a gap — and that filling it required something more rigorous and more serious than the hackathons and coding competitions that already existed.

The first IBCOL HK qualifying round in 2022 had 22 teams. That number felt both gratifyingly large and precarious — we had no idea whether it would grow or collapse in subsequent years. It grew. And the growth taught us things that no amount of planning could have predicted.

Seven Things We Have Learned

01
Rigour is the product, not the barrier
When we first designed the IBCOL HK format — written research paper, live presentation, expert judging panel, strict marking criteria — several people warned us that Hong Kong students would find it too demanding and participation would be low. The opposite happened. The rigour was exactly what drew students in. Students who want a superficial experience have plenty of options. The students who were looking for something that would genuinely test them had almost nothing before KBI HK. Rigour was not a barrier to participation; it was the reason for participation.
02
Community is infrastructure, not decoration
In the early years, we treated community events — networking nights, seminars, meet-ups — as add-ons: nice to have, but secondary to the competition. We were wrong. The community infrastructure turned out to be load-bearing. The connections formed at our networking events produced cross-institution teams. The seminars produced participants who would never have registered without the introductory content. The alumni network produced judges, mentors, and sponsors. A competition without community is a one-off event. A competition with community is an institution.
03
Secondary school students are seriously underestimated
When we added the Schoolers Stream in 2025, we expected secondary school participation to be modest and the standard to be notably lower than tertiary students. Both assumptions were wrong. Secondary school teams competed with a seriousness and creativity that surprised every judge panel. The problem was never student capability — it was access. Secondary school students had never been invited into these spaces before. Once they were, they showed up fully. This is perhaps the single most important thing we have learned, and it is shaping everything we do in 2026.
04
Volunteer-run does not mean amateur
KBI HK has been run entirely by volunteers since its founding. Every committee member, every track director, every event organiser has been doing this alongside their full-time studies or careers. This is often cited as a limitation — and in some ways it is. But it is also a feature. The people who run KBI HK do so because they believe in it, not because they are paid to. That authenticity is visible to participants, sponsors, and partners in ways that are difficult to manufacture. The challenge is not to professionalise the organisation to the point of losing that quality — it is to build enough structure that volunteer effort is sustainable and not dependent on any single person's heroic output.
05
Adding a new track is harder than it looks
We added IDSOL in 2023 and IQCOL in 2024. Both additions were successful — but both were also significantly more operationally complex than we had anticipated. A new competition track is not just a new set of judging criteria. It is a new judge network, a new participant pipeline, a new set of preparatory resources, a new international relationship, and a new body of subject matter expertise that the committee needs to develop. We would do both additions again — but we would give ourselves more runway and more committee bandwidth before launch.
06
Institutional relationships compound over time
Our relationships with HKU, CUHK, HKUST, PolyU, and Cyberport have all deepened progressively over the years. The first time we asked an institution for a venue, we were a small student group with an interesting idea. By the third or fourth time, we were a known quantity with a track record and a community that the institution valued. The investment in building those relationships — showing up consistently, delivering quality, communicating clearly — compounded into structural support that no one-off pitch could have produced. This applies equally to sponsors, judges, and media partners.
07
The students remember it differently than you do
At our 2023 Alumni Reunion, we heard from participants across eight cohort years. Almost universally, the things they remembered most were not the things we had spent the most time on. They remembered specific moments of pressure — a judge's question they hadn't prepared for, a dataset they couldn't crack for the first two hours, a presentation that went better than expected. They remembered the people they met through KBI HK who became long-term colleagues. What we had optimised for — the quality of the judging, the rigour of the marking criteria, the prestige of the venue — was almost invisible to them. What mattered was the experience of discovering their own capacity under real conditions. That is the thing worth protecting above all else.

What We Got Wrong

It would be dishonest to write a nine-year reflection without acknowledging the failures. There were several.

We expanded too fast in 2023, adding both a new competition track and a major community event in the same cycle without sufficient committee bandwidth. The result was a year in which several things were delivered at a lower standard than we would have accepted in other years. We recovered — but the experience reinforced a lesson that organisations often have to learn the hard way: growing faster than your operational capacity is not ambition, it is risk.

We were also slow to formalise our sponsorship model. For the first several years, sponsorship was handled informally through existing relationships, with no documented structure, no consistent benefits package, and no multi-year strategy. This left money on the table and made it harder to build the kind of sustained sponsor relationships that would have given the chapter more financial stability. The 2026 operational restructure addresses this directly — but we should have addressed it in 2023.

And we underinvested in documentation and institutional memory. When committee members completed their terms and transitioned out, knowledge left with them. We have made progress on this — the 2026 restructure includes formal handover processes and documentation requirements — but the cost of not having done this earlier has been real and recurring.

"Nine years in, the thing I am most proud of is not any single event or any particular result. It is that there is now a community in Hong Kong that takes blockchain, data science, and quantum computing education seriously — and that community did not exist before KBI HK."

— Dr. Michelle Lam, Chapter Director, KBI Hong Kong

Looking at 2026 and Beyond

The 2026 programme cycle is the most ambitious we have ever designed. Three competition tracks. Two formal participation streams. A restructured committee with dedicated role ownership. A formal volunteer leadership programme. A multi-year sponsorship strategy. And a community programme that has grown from quarterly events to a year-round calendar.

None of this would have been possible without the nine years of institution-building that preceded it. The relationships, the track record, the alumni network, the operational lessons — all of it is load-bearing for what we are attempting in 2026.

What I hope for in the next nine years is not that KBI HK becomes bigger — though I expect it will. It is that it remains serious. That the students who participate continue to do so because they want to be genuinely challenged, not because they want a line on their CV. That the standard of the work we ask for continues to be a real standard — one that requires real effort, produces real learning, and means something when it is achieved.

That is what we built this for. And that is what we are still building.
