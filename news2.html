Why Blockchain Education
Matters for Hong Kong Students

Arjun Sharma
Blockchain Engineer & KBI HK Mentor
28 April 2026
6 min read

There is a persistent misconception about blockchain: that it is primarily a technology for cryptocurrency speculation, and therefore unsuitable as a subject for serious academic study. This misunderstanding has, over the past several years, prevented a generation of Hong Kong students from engaging with one of the most genuinely significant advances in distributed computing since the internet itself.

As someone who builds blockchain infrastructure professionally — and who mentors students preparing for IBCOL through KBI HK — I want to make the case plainly: blockchain literacy is not a niche skill. It is quickly becoming a foundational one.

The Hype Has Cleared. What Remains Is Real.

The years between 2020 and 2023 were, to put it charitably, chaotic. NFT speculation, exchange collapses, and relentless media cycles around token prices created a cultural environment in which "blockchain" was almost synonymous with financial risk and unserious behaviour.

That period has passed. What has emerged in its wake is a cleaner, more substantive conversation about what blockchain actually is: a solution to a deep and longstanding problem in computer science — the problem of trust between parties who do not know or necessarily trust each other.

"The question was never 'can we make digital tokens?' The question was always: can two parties transact, share data, or coordinate — without a trusted intermediary? That question is still worth answering."

— Dr. Michelle Lam, HKUST Research Fellow

This is the question at the heart of every serious blockchain application — from supply chain provenance and digital identity through to tokenised real-world assets and cross-border settlement. These are not hypothetical use cases. They are live deployments, many of them in Hong Kong and across Asia.

The Hong Kong Context

Hong Kong occupies a genuinely unusual position. As a global financial centre with a strong rule-of-law tradition and sophisticated regulatory institutions, it is both well-positioned to lead in blockchain-adjacent finance — and deeply incentivised to do so.

The Hong Kong Monetary Authority, the Securities and Futures Commission, and a growing cluster of licensed digital asset businesses have all made explicit commitments to developing a regulated, legitimate blockchain ecosystem in the city. Meanwhile, Hong Kong's universities — HKU, HKUST, CUHK, PolyU — are producing research output in cryptographic protocols and distributed systems that ranks among the best globally.

Against this backdrop, a student who understands how consensus protocols work, what smart contracts can and cannot do, and how to evaluate a blockchain proposal critically — is not someone with a niche interest. They are someone who has equipped themselves for a sector that is actively building in this city.

Why Competition-Based Learning Accelerates Understanding

Textbooks and courses can explain blockchain. Competitions make you understand it.

The difference is accountability. When you're preparing an IBCOL submission — a written project paper that will be read and critiqued by a panel of academics and industry professionals — you cannot hide behind vague statements. You have to know what you're talking about. You have to be able to defend your technical choices under live questioning.

This is, in my experience as a mentor, where real learning happens. Not in the months of preparation, but in the ten minutes of Q&A with a judge who spotted the flaw in your consensus mechanism design.

What IBCOL Competition Experience Develops

Technical depth in distributed ledger design · Critical evaluation of real blockchain proposals · Communication of complex technical concepts to non-specialist audiences · Collaborative project management under time pressure · Research methodology and citation practice · Resilience in adversarial intellectual environments

The Curriculum Gap

Hong Kong's school and university curricula have made genuine progress in incorporating computing and data science content. Blockchain, however, remains conspicuously absent from most programmes — for understandable reasons. The field moves fast, educators are understandably cautious about associating their curriculum with volatile assets, and quality teaching resources are scarce.

This is precisely where KBI HK and programmes like IBCOL fill a gap that formal education cannot easily address. We are not a curriculum replacement — we are a curriculum extension. Our workshops, clinics, and competition preparation materials give students access to practitioner knowledge and intellectual rigour that sits beyond the classroom.

A secondary school student who participates in the Blockchain Foundations Clinic and then competes in IBCOL has, by the end of the process, a practical understanding of distributed ledgers that most computer science undergraduates do not possess.

Where to Start

For students who are curious but uncertain where to begin, my practical advice is:

  • Attend the Blockchain Foundations Clinic — no prior knowledge required, and the Q&A alone is worth the morning
  • Register interest for ETO HK 2026 — even if you're not sure which track to compete in, registration gets you the prospectus and access to team formation support
  • Join the KBI community — the quarterly meet-ups are the fastest way to find teammates, mentors, and peers who are serious about this
  • Read critically — not crypto news sites, but academic papers and regulatory publications. The HKMA website alone contains more useful blockchain content than most courses

Blockchain is not going away. Hong Kong is actively building in this space. The students who take the time to understand it properly — at a technical and conceptual level — are positioning themselves for opportunities that will be genuinely difficult to access without that foundation.

The hype is over. The work begins now.
