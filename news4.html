Why Quantum Computing
Education Cannot Wait

Raymond Cheung
Quantum Physics Adviser, KBI HK
18 April 2026
7 min read

In October 2024, KBI Hong Kong ran its first Quantum Computing Introduction Seminar Series — four sessions across HKU and HKUST, drawing a combined 150 attendees. By March 2025, 28 teams had entered the IQCOL Hong Kong qualifying round. In less than six months, a subject most Hong Kong students had never encountered in a classroom had become one of the most competitive tracks in our annual programme.

That trajectory is not an accident. It reflects something deeper: when students are given structured, credible access to quantum computing concepts, they engage — and they engage seriously. The problem is that most students in Hong Kong, and indeed across most of Asia, are not getting that access. Not in school. Rarely in university. Almost never before the age of eighteen.

This article makes the case for why that needs to change, and why it needs to change now.

"The students who engage with quantum computing today are not learning a future skill. They are learning the present reality of where computation is heading — and getting ahead of almost everyone else."

— Raymond Cheung, Quantum Physics Adviser, KBI HK

The Hardware Window Is Closing

For most of the last decade, quantum computing was a research curiosity — theoretically powerful, practically limited. Error rates were high. Qubit counts were low. The gap between what quantum machines could do in principle and what they could do in practice was enormous. It was reasonable, in that context, to treat quantum education as something to defer.

That window is closing. IBM, Google, and a growing number of Chinese national labs have each demonstrated quantum processors with error rates approaching practical utility thresholds. IBM's roadmap targets fault-tolerant quantum computing by 2029. Google's Willow chip, announced in late 2024, demonstrated a computational task that would take classical supercomputers an estimated 10 septillion years to complete.

These are not marketing claims — they are peer-reviewed results published in Nature. The transition from "research curiosity" to "applied technology" is no longer measured in decades. It is measured in years.

A student who begins studying quantum computing at university today will graduate into a job market where quantum literacy is already a differentiator. A student who begins at secondary school will have a meaningful head start on most of their future colleagues — including those at elite institutions that are only now adding introductory quantum modules to undergraduate curricula.

What "Quantum Literacy" Actually Means

There is a common misconception that quantum computing education requires advanced physics. It does — eventually. But introductory quantum literacy does not begin with the Schrödinger equation. It begins with conceptual frameworks that secondary students can engage with meaningfully.

At KBI HK, we define quantum literacy across three levels:

  • Conceptual literacy — Understanding superposition, entanglement, and interference as phenomena; appreciating why qubits behave differently from classical bits; grasping the computational implications without requiring a physics degree
  • Algorithmic literacy — Familiarity with foundational quantum algorithms (Shor's, Grover's, QAOA); understanding where quantum speedup applies and where it does not; ability to reason about problem types suited to quantum approaches
  • Practical literacy — Using frameworks like IBM Qiskit or Google Cirq to write and run quantum circuits; interpreting output from real quantum hardware; engaging with quantum noise and error correction at a practitioner level

The vast majority of students — even those pursuing careers in technology — will only ever need the first two levels. And both are accessible to motivated secondary school students with solid mathematics backgrounds. We have seen this firsthand: our 2024 seminar series delivered conceptual and algorithmic content to mixed groups of secondary and university students, and the secondary students kept pace entirely.

Why Olympiad-Style Competition Accelerates Learning

One of the most consistent findings in educational research on high-achieving students is that structured, competitive contexts accelerate learning in ways that classroom instruction alone cannot match. The mechanism is not competition for its own sake — it is the combination of a real deadline, a real audience, and real stakes.

When a student is preparing a quantum computing project for IQCOL, they are not doing so for a grade that disappears into a transcript. They are doing so for a presentation in front of expert judges, in a room with their peers, with the possibility of advancing to an international final. That context changes how they study. It changes how deeply they read. It changes how willing they are to ask for help and push through difficult material.

This is precisely why the IQCOL HK qualifying process requires teams to produce a written research paper alongside their live presentation. The paper forces clarity of thinking. You cannot bluff your way through a written explanation of why your quantum algorithm is appropriate for the problem you have chosen. You have to understand it. And in the process of making yourself understand it well enough to write it clearly, you learn it at a depth that most university courses never achieve.

"The question is not whether students are capable of engaging with quantum computing. We have seen that they are. The question is whether we give them the opportunity."

— Raymond Cheung, KBI HK Quantum Adviser

The Cost of Waiting

The argument for deferring quantum education usually comes in one of two forms. The first is pedagogical: "Students should master classical computing before moving to quantum." The second is practical: "There are no quantum jobs yet, so why prioritise it now?"

Both arguments have a surface plausibility that dissolves under scrutiny.

The pedagogical argument assumes a sequential model of technological literacy that does not reflect how the field actually works. Quantum computing does not replace classical computing — it complements it. Understanding where quantum approaches are useful requires understanding classical computing, yes. But it does not require mastering it. Secondary students learning Python or R already have sufficient classical computing context to engage meaningfully with introductory quantum concepts. The two are not mutually exclusive.

The practical argument misunderstands the timeline. Students who are seventeen today will be entering the workforce in 2030 or 2031. The cohort of professionals with quantum literacy — genuinely useful, applied quantum literacy — will be extremely small at that point. The students who began studying quantum computing in 2025 or 2026 will be among the most prepared people in that labour market. Waiting until "the jobs exist" means training people who will arrive late to a field where early movers have a structural advantage.

What KBI HK Is Doing

KBI Hong Kong has made quantum computing a structural part of its programme offering, not an elective add-on. IQCOL HK is a fully integrated track within ETO HK 2026, with the same rigour, the same support infrastructure, and the same international pathway as IBCOL and IDSOL.

We are also continuing our preparatory seminar series. The Quantum Computing Primer Workshop in September 2026 is designed specifically for students who are new to the field — no physics background required, just mathematical curiosity and a willingness to engage with unfamiliar ideas.

If you are a student, educator, or parent reading this: the time to start is now. Not after the next cohort. Not when quantum becomes "mainstream." Now — because the students who start now will still be ahead when mainstream arrives.
