Alumni Spotlight:
From IBCOL to Research Fellowship

KBI Hong Kong Editorial
Community Series
14 March 2026
4 min read

In our ongoing Alumni Spotlight series, we speak with KBI HK community members who have gone on to build careers, research agendas, and ventures shaped — in part — by their time in KBI's programmes. This edition features Dr. Michelle Lam, now a Research Fellow in Distributed Systems at HKUST, who competed in IBCOL as an undergraduate and has been involved in the KBI HK community ever since.

Dr. Michelle Lam
Research Fellow in Distributed Systems · HKUST Department of Computer Science & Engineering
Dr. Lam completed her undergraduate degree in Computer Science at HKUST, where she first encountered KBI HK through a colleague's recommendation before an IBCOL competition cycle. She went on to complete a PhD at Oxford on consensus protocol design, returning to HKUST as a Research Fellow in 2024. She now serves as an IBCOL international judge and mentors KBI HK competition teams.
IBCOL Alumni HKUST Distributed Systems KBI HK Mentor

A Decade in the Making

Michelle's path from first-year undergraduate to Research Fellow spans roughly a decade — and KBI HK is a thread that runs through almost all of it.

2018
First IBCOL at ETO HK — Silver Award
Michelle's team submitted a blockchain-based land registry proposal for emerging markets. "We didn't win, but the judges' feedback was more valuable than any lecture I'd attended."
2019
IBCOL International Finals — Gold
Returned with a substantially revised project and took Gold at the international finals. "That year, I learned how to take criticism, rebuild from it, and present under real pressure."
2019–2023
DPhil, Computer Science — University of Oxford
Focused on formal verification of BFT consensus protocols. Published three papers in top-tier venues. "My IBCOL project was the seed. The question I explored at Oxford was directly descended from it."
2024–Present
Research Fellow, HKUST · KBI HK Mentor & Judge
Returned to Hong Kong to build a distributed systems research group at HKUST. Joined the IBCOL International Judging Panel. Mentors three KBI HK competition teams per cycle.

In Her Own Words

We sat down with Dr. Lam at HKUST ahead of the March 2026 Community Meet-Up, where she is giving one of five lightning talks. Here's a condensed version of that conversation.

Q
What did IBCOL give you that your degree programme didn't?

Accountability. In a course, you write an assignment and a professor grades it. In IBCOL, you write a paper, present it, and then a panel of experts tells you, in front of an audience, exactly where your reasoning breaks down. That's terrifying the first time. By the second time, it's the most valuable feedback mechanism I know of. My DPhil supervisor actually commented that I had unusually good instincts for identifying the weakest link in my own argument — I think that came directly from those IBCOL Q&A sessions.

Q
Was there a specific moment where you realised blockchain was going to be your research focus?

The 2018 judging feedback, actually. One of the judges — a very senior academic — asked us a question we couldn't answer: "How does your system handle Byzantine faults in the validation layer?" I had no idea what Byzantine faults were. I went home that evening and read everything I could find. Three months later, I had the seeds of what would become my thesis. The moment you encounter a question you genuinely cannot answer, and it genuinely bothers you — that's where research starts.

Q
What do you look for when you're judging IBCOL submissions now?

Intellectual honesty, above everything. I can teach a team the technical content — I can't teach them to be honest about the limits of what they've built. The projects I remember, and recommend for awards, are the ones where students know exactly what their system doesn't do and can articulate why that's a meaningful constraint rather than a failure. That maturity — being rigorous about your own work's boundaries — is genuinely rare, and it's what separates a competition winner from everyone else.

Q
What would you say to a secondary school student in Hong Kong who is curious about blockchain but doesn't know where to start?

Start by ignoring the price charts. Completely. Blockchain is a computer science problem, not a financial product. Read the Bitcoin whitepaper — it's nine pages and it's one of the clearest pieces of technical writing I've encountered. Then go to a KBI event. The community here is genuinely unusual — you'll meet people who actually want to discuss the ideas, not just speculate on tokens. And when ETO HK opens, enter. You don't need to win. You need to ask questions you can't answer yet.

"The moment you encounter a question you genuinely cannot answer, and it genuinely bothers you — that's where research starts."

— Dr. Michelle Lam, HKUST Research Fellow & KBI HK Alumni

What's Next

Dr. Lam is currently leading a research group at HKUST focused on the formal verification of next-generation consensus protocols — work she describes as "finding the limits of what current blockchain architectures can actually guarantee, and then trying to push those limits." She expects to publish two new papers in 2026 and is in early discussions about a collaborative research project with KBI HK's academic partner network.

She will be speaking at the KBI Community Meet-Up on 22 March 2026, where she'll give a lightning talk on her journey from competition to research. She is also serving as a judge at this year's ETO HK presentation day.

"I keep coming back to KBI because the community is serious," she says. "Everyone here is trying to understand something. That's not as common as you'd hope."
